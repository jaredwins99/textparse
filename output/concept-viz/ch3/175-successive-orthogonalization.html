Successive Orthogonalization

Concept 175 ESL §3.2.3 Algorithm 3.1 Gram-Schmidt for Regression

Multiple regression is just a sequence of simple regressions. Orthogonalize each predictor against all previous ones, then regress y on the residuals. The coefficients are the same as OLS — this procedure reveals why.

Benefits & Tradeoffs
What does this decomposition buy you?
Benefits
  • Reveals what multiple regression actually does. "When the inputs are orthogonal, [multiple regression coefficients] are the same as univariate estimates. Otherwise, they must be adjusted for correlated inputs" (ESL §3.2.3, p. 54). This procedure shows exactly how that adjustment works.
  • Connects to the QR decomposition. Algorithm 3.1 IS Gram-Schmidt. "The result of this algorithm is... the QR decomposition of X" (ESL §3.2.3, p. 55). The conceptual story and the computational tool are the same thing.
  • Explains collinearity instability. When predictors are highly correlated, the residual zj after orthogonalization is tiny. Dividing by a tiny residual amplifies noise — you can see why correlated features produce wild coefficients.
  • Foundation for many Ch3 algorithms. Ridge, LAR, forward stagewise, boosting, and PLS are all variations on this theme: project, shrink, repeat. Understanding this procedure is the key to the rest of the chapter.
Tradeoffs
  • Sensitive to predictor ordering. The intermediate residuals zj depend on which variables were orthogonalized first. Different orderings produce different z's (though the final regression gives the same β̂).
  • Not how OLS is actually computed. In practice, the QR decomposition uses Householder reflections (numerically stable), not classical Gram-Schmidt (which can lose orthogonality). This is a pedagogical tool, not a computational recipe.
  • The last coefficient is special. "The multiple regression coefficient β̂p ... is the univariate regression coefficient of y on zp" (ESL §3.2.3, p. 54) — only the last variable's coefficient has the simple interpretation.
"This recipe shows that the multiple regression coefficient β̂j represents the additional contribution of xj on y, after xj has been adjusted for x0, x1, ..., xj-1." — ESL §3.2.3, p. 54
Watch the Orthogonalization: Peeling Off Projections
Multiple regression with 3 predictors. Each step projects a variable onto the previous ones and keeps only the residual — the part that's genuinely new information.
0Setup: Raw Data
Two predictors x1 and x2, correlated at r = 0.80. Response y depends on both. We want to find multiple regression coefficients β̂1 and β̂2.
1z₁ = x₁ (first predictor stays)
The first predictor needs no adjustment — it defines the starting direction. z1 = x1.
2Project x₂ onto x₁, keep the residual z₂
Remove from x2 everything that x1 already explains. z2 = x2 − (x₁ᵀx₂/x₁ᵀx₁)x1. This is the "new information" in x2. When correlation is high, z2 is tiny.
3Regress y on z₂ → this IS β̂₂
The coefficient from regressing y on the residual z2 is exactly the multiple regression coefficient β̂2. No matrix inversion needed — just simple regressions and subtraction.
Correlation r(x₁, x₂): 0.80
At high correlation, z₂ is nearly zero — dividing by it amplifies noise. This is why collinearity makes coefficients unstable: the "new information" in each predictor is vanishingly small.
Correlation Drives Instability
As corr(x₁, x₂) increases, the orthogonal residual z₂ shrinks. The coefficient β̂₂ has to divide by ||z₂||², so noise gets amplified. Drag the slider and watch the variance explode.

||z₂|| — how much "new information" x₂ adds

Var(β̂₂) — coefficient variance via orthogonalization

The variance of β̂j is σ²/||zj||². When predictors are orthogonal, ||zj|| = ||xj|| and variance is minimal. As correlation → 1, ||zj|| → 0 and variance → ∞. This is the geometric explanation of collinearity: there's no independent direction left to estimate.
Bridge to Ridge: Shrinking After Orthogonalization
Ridge regression applies shrinkage in the orthogonalized directions. Directions with tiny ||zj|| get shrunk the most — exactly the noisy ones.
Ridge λ: 0.00

OLS vs Ridge coefficients

Shrinkage factor d²/(d² + λ) per SVD direction

In the SVD basis, ridge multiplies each direction's coefficient by dj²/(dj² + λ). Directions where the data has little variance (small dj) — the ones that make collinearity dangerous — get shrunk toward zero. Ridge tames exactly the instability that successive orthogonalization reveals.
Statistical Guarantees
Exact equivalence to OLS YES
Algorithm 3.1 produces exactly the same β̂ as (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy. It's a different path to the same destination — useful for understanding, not for computation.
QR decomposition connection YES
The zj residuals form the columns of Q (after normalization), and the projection coefficients form R. This IS Gram-Schmidt, which IS the QR factorization. (ESL §3.2.3, p. 55)
Variance = σ²/||zj||² YES
The variance of the j-th multiple regression coefficient depends only on how much independent information zj carries. This gives the geometric explanation of standard errors. (ESL §3.2.3, p. 55)
Order dependence PARTIAL
Intermediate residuals zj change with predictor ordering. But the final β̂ is order-invariant — every ordering produces the same OLS solution. Only the interpretive decomposition changes.
Numerical stability CAUTION
Classical Gram-Schmidt can lose orthogonality in finite precision. Modified Gram-Schmidt is better; Householder reflections (used in practice) are best. This procedure is for understanding, not production code.
Formulas — Reference
Algorithm 3.1 — Regression by Successive Orthogonalization

1. Initialize: $\mathbf{z}_0 = \mathbf{x}_0 = \mathbf{1}$ (intercept)
2. For $j = 1, 2, \ldots, p$: regress $\mathbf{x}_j$ on $\mathbf{z}_0, \mathbf{z}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{z}_{j-1}$ to get residual $\mathbf{z}_j$
3. Regress $\mathbf{y}$ on the residual $\mathbf{z}_p$ to give $\hat\beta_p$

$$\hat\beta_p = \frac{\langle \mathbf{z}_p, \mathbf{y} \rangle}{\langle \mathbf{z}_p, \mathbf{z}_p \rangle}$$

The multiple regression coefficient of the last variable is just a simple regression of y on the orthogonalized residual.

$$\text{Var}(\hat\beta_p) = \frac{\sigma^2}{\langle \mathbf{z}_p, \mathbf{z}_p \rangle} = \frac{\sigma^2}{\|\mathbf{z}_p\|^2}$$

Variance depends on how much independent information zp carries. High correlation → small ||zp|| → large variance. This is the VIF in geometric form: VIFj = 1/(1 − R²j) = ||xj||²/||zj||².
