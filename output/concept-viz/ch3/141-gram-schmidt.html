Book Quote

"Algorithm 3.1 is known as the Gram-Schmidt procedure for multiple regression, and is also a form of the QR decomposition for X." — ESL, Section 3.2.3
"The result of this algorithm is \(\hat{\beta}_j = \langle \mathbf{z}_j, \mathbf{y} \rangle / \langle \mathbf{z}_j, \mathbf{z}_j \rangle\). Re-expressing, the multiple regression coefficient of \(x_j\) is the simple regression of \(y\) on \(z_j\)." — ESL, Section 3.2.3

Guarantees

✓
Produces Orthonormal Basis
Each qⱼ is unit length, and ⟨qᵢ, qⱼ⟩ = 0 for i ≠ j. Span is preserved at every step.
✓
Isolates Marginal Contribution
zⱼ captures the component of xⱼ orthogonal to all previous predictors — pure marginal information.
✓
Coefficient Invariance
The last coefficient β̂ₚ is identical whether computed by GS or by full OLS on all p predictors.
✓
Reveals Collinearity
If ‖zⱼ‖ ≈ 0, predictor j is nearly a linear combination of earlier predictors — the algorithm exposes this.
~
Order-Dependent
The basis {q₁, …, qₚ} depends on the ordering of predictors. The final β̂ values are the same, but intermediate zⱼ change.
~
Numerical Stability
Classical GS is unstable for nearly-dependent vectors; Modified GS (MGS) fixes this and is preferred in practice.
