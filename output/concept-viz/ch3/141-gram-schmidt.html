Benefits & Tradeoffs

Isolates each predictor's unique contribution. After orthogonalizing, "the multiple regression coefficient \(\hat\beta_j\) represents the additional contribution of \(x_j\) on \(y\), after \(x_j\) has been adjusted for \(x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{j-1}\)." (ESL §3.2.3)
Produces the QR decomposition. "Algorithm 3.1 is known as the Gram-Schmidt procedure for multiple regression, and is also a form of the QR decomposition for \(X\)." (ESL §3.2.3)
Reveals collinearity. If a residual \(\|z_j\|\) is near zero, that predictor is nearly a linear combination of earlier ones — the algorithm exposes this numerically.
Order-dependent. The intermediate residuals \(z_j\) change depending on the ordering of predictors. The final \(\hat\beta\) values are invariant, but the orthonormal basis \(\{q_j\}\) is not.
Numerically unstable (classical version). Classical Gram-Schmidt accumulates rounding errors for nearly-dependent vectors. Modified Gram-Schmidt (MGS) is preferred in practice.
"The result of this algorithm is \(\hat{\beta}_j = \langle \mathbf{z}_j, \mathbf{y} \rangle / \langle \mathbf{z}_j, \mathbf{z}_j \rangle\). Re-expressing, the multiple regression coefficient of \(x_j\) is the simple regression of \(y\) on \(z_j\)." — ESL, Section 3.2.3

Statistical Guarantees

✓
Produces Orthonormal Basis
Each q_j is unit length, and ⟨q_i, q_j⟩ = 0 for i ≠ j. Span is preserved at every step.
✓
Isolates Marginal Contribution
z_j captures the component of x_j orthogonal to all previous predictors — pure marginal information.
✓
Coefficient Invariance
The last coefficient β̂_p is identical whether computed by GS or by full OLS on all p predictors.
✓
Reveals Collinearity
If ‖z_j‖ ≈ 0, predictor j is nearly a linear combination of earlier predictors — the algorithm exposes this.
~
Order-Dependent
The basis {q_1, …, q_p} depends on the ordering of predictors. The final β̂ values are the same, but intermediate z_j change.
~
Numerical Stability
Classical GS is unstable for nearly-dependent vectors; Modified GS (MGS) fixes this and is preferred in practice.

Formulas (Reference)

Gram-Schmidt procedure (Algorithm 3.1 in ESL):

\[ z_0 = x_0 = \mathbf{1} \] \[ \text{For } j = 1, \ldots, p: \quad z_j = x_j - \sum_{k=0}^{j-1} \frac{\langle x_j, z_k \rangle}{\langle z_k, z_k \rangle} z_k, \quad q_j = \frac{z_j}{\|z_j\|} \] \[ \hat{\beta}_p = \frac{\langle z_p, \mathbf{y} \rangle}{\langle z_p, z_p \rangle} \]
