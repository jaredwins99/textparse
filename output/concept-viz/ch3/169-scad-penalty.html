SCAD Penalty

What this fixes vs. vanilla Lasso

Benefits

Eliminates shrinkage bias for large coefficients. Lasso penalizes all coefficients equally, so truly large signals get shrunk toward zero. SCAD tapers the penalty to zero for large coefficients -- they pass through unpenalized.
Oracle property (Fan & Li 2001): asymptotically performs as well as if you knew the true support set. Small coefficients are zeroed; large coefficients are estimated as if unpenalized.
Continuous solution in the data -- unlike hard thresholding, SCAD doesn't have jumps.

Tradeoffs

Non-convex penalty. The objective function has local minima, so optimization is harder -- no guarantee of finding the global optimum without careful initialization.
Extra tuning parameter (a, typically 3.7) beyond lambda. Though Fan & Li argue a=3.7 works well in practice.
"One can modify the lasso penalty function so that larger coefficients are shrunken less severely." -- ESL, Section 3.8.5

Penalty shape comparison: SCAD vs. L1 vs. L2

The key insight is the shape. L1 (Lasso) grows linearly forever -- large coefficients pay a large price. L2 (Ridge) grows quadratically -- even worse for large coefficients. SCAD flattens out: once a coefficient is large enough, the penalty stops growing. That is how it avoids shrinkage bias.

Formula reference

\( p'_\lambda(|\beta|) = \lambda \left[ \mathbf{1}(|\beta| \le \lambda) + \frac{(a\lambda - |\beta|)_+}{(a-1)\lambda}\,\mathbf{1}(|\beta| > \lambda) \right], \quad a > 2 \)

Three zones: (1) \(|\beta| \le \lambda\): full L1 penalty, same as Lasso. (2) \(\lambda < |\beta| \le a\lambda\): penalty tapers smoothly toward a constant. (3) \(|\beta| > a\lambda\): constant penalty -- no additional shrinkage. Fan & Li (2001) recommend \(a = 3.7\).
