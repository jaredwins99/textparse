L2Boost

Concept 165 ESL 3.8 Estimation / Boosting Buhlmann & Hothorn 2008

Forward stagewise regression seen through the lens of gradient boosting: each round fits the residual with the best single predictor and adds a small fraction of it. The residual shrinks incrementally, round by round.

Benefits & Tradeoffs
What does the boosting perspective buy you?
Benefits
  • Connects regression to boosting. "Buhlmann and Hothorn (2008) refer to the same procedure as 'L2boost', since it can be viewed as a form of boosting using squared-error loss and linear base learners" (ESL 3.8, p. 88). This reframes forward stagewise as functional gradient descent.
  • Stopping iteration M acts as regularization. Early stopping prevents overfitting without needing an explicit penalty parameter. M is the analog of 1/λ in lasso -- small M = strong regularization.
  • Naturally selects variables. Each round picks only the most useful predictor. Irrelevant variables are rarely or never selected, producing implicit sparsity.
  • Generalizes to nonlinear learners. Replace "best single predictor" with a regression tree and you get gradient boosting machines (Friedman 2001). L2Boost is the simplest instance of a hugely successful family.
Tradeoffs
  • Biased unless M → ∞. Stopping early means coefficients have not converged to their OLS values. This bias is intentional (it reduces variance), but you must cross-validate M.
  • Slow convergence. Like FSε, L2Boost needs many rounds to reach the OLS solution. The small step size ε is what makes it regularize, but also what makes it slow.
  • Step size ε and stopping M are coupled hyperparameters. Smaller ε needs more iterations for the same fit. In practice, fix ε small and cross-validate M.
  • Can overfit if M is too large. Given enough rounds, L2Boost will eventually interpolate the training data. The regularization benefit only holds when stopped early.

Step Size Matters: How ε Shapes the Path
Side-by-side comparison of L2Boost at different step sizes. Smaller ε produces smoother, more regularized paths.
At ε = 0.02 (thin solid), paths are smooth and track the lasso. At ε = 0.50 (thick dashed), paths are jagged -- each step is a large jump. The number of iterations to reach a given residual level scales as O(1/ε). Smaller ε gives better regularization at higher computational cost.
Statistical Guarantees
Prediction consistency YES
With appropriate early stopping (M chosen by CV), L2Boost achieves prediction error that converges to the best linear approximation. Buhlmann & Yu (2003) establish consistency for boosting with componentwise learners.
Implicit sparsity YES
Irrelevant variables (true coefficient = 0) are rarely selected. At early stopping points, the effective model is sparse without an explicit L1 penalty.
Low variance YES
The small step size means each iteration adds only a tiny perturbation to the fit. This makes the procedure resistant to noise -- high variance in the data is absorbed slowly.
Path = FS0 as ε → 0 YES
L2Boost with coordinate projections as base learners IS forward stagewise regression. In the limit ε → 0, it traces the FS0 = lasso path.
Consistent (M → ∞, n → ∞) YES
When both the number of boosting rounds and sample size grow, L2Boost converges to the true regression function.
Unbiased NO (M < inf)
Early stopping introduces bias -- coefficients have not reached their OLS values. This is the bias-variance tradeoff in action: you accept bias to buy variance reduction.
Formulas -- Reference
$$j^* = \arg\max_j \bigl|\langle \mathbf{x}_j,\, \mathbf{r}^{(m-1)} \rangle\bigr|$$

Find the "weak learner" (predictor) most correlated with the current residual.

$$\hat\beta_{j^*}^{(m)} = \hat\beta_{j^*}^{(m-1)} + \varepsilon \cdot \frac{\langle \mathbf{x}_{j^*},\, \mathbf{r}^{(m-1)} \rangle}{\langle \mathbf{x}_{j^*},\, \mathbf{x}_{j^*} \rangle}$$

Update that coefficient by ε times the univariate OLS coefficient of the residual on xj*. This is gradient descent in function space with squared-error loss.

$$\mathbf{r}^{(m)} = \mathbf{r}^{(m-1)} - \varepsilon \, \frac{\langle \mathbf{x}_{j^*},\, \mathbf{r}^{(m-1)} \rangle}{\langle \mathbf{x}_{j^*},\, \mathbf{x}_{j^*} \rangle} \, \mathbf{x}_{j^*}$$

Update residuals by subtracting ε of the projection onto the selected predictor. With ε = 1 this is exactly OLS on one variable; with ε small, it's a fractional step.

This is Algorithm 3.4 in ESL (p. 86) viewed through the boosting lens. The "base learner" at each round is a single-variable linear fit to the residual. With ε small and early stopping at M, the procedure regularizes by limiting the total number of greedy steps. Cross-validate M for optimal prediction.
