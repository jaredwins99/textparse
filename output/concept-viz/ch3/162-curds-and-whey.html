ESL §3.7 · ID 162 · Breiman & Friedman 1997 · Category: Comparison / Aggregation

Curds and Whey

Benefits

  • Smooth shrinkage instead of hard truncation -- no need to choose a rank M. Each canonical direction gets its own data-driven shrinkage factor
    "Curds and whey involves shrinkage of the canonical variates between X and Y, a smooth version of reduced rank regression." -- ESL §3.7
  • Guaranteed to reduce MSE vs OLS -- it's a James-Stein-type estimator
  • More robust than RRR when the true rank is unknown -- doesn't require guessing a cutoff
  • Adapts automatically to the strength of each canonical direction: keeps strong directions, shrinks weak ones

Tradeoffs

  • Biased estimator -- shrinkage always introduces bias (trades it for lower variance)
  • Requires estimating canonical correlations first, which are upward-biased in small samples
  • When the true coefficient matrix IS exactly low-rank, RRR with the correct M will beat Curds & Whey
  • Shrinkage formula assumes sample canonical correlations are reasonable estimates of population values -- breaks down when n/p is very small
Shrinkage Profile: How Much Each Direction Gets Kept

Gold curve: Curds & Whey shrinkage factor s(rho) as a function of canonical correlation. When rho is high, the direction is mostly kept (s near 1). When rho is low, it's heavily shrunk (s near 0). Compare with RRR's hard step function (orange dashed) -- C&W smoothly interpolates. As n/p grows, C&W approaches OLS (less shrinkage needed).

Formula Reference
\(\displaystyle \hat{\mathbf{Y}}^{c\&w} = \mathbf{X}\hat{\mathbf{B}}^{\text{OLS}}\,\mathbf{S}^{c\&w}\)
\(\displaystyle s_k^{c\&w} = \frac{\rho_k^2}{\rho_k^2 + \frac{p}{n}(1 - \rho_k^2)}\)

Each canonical direction k gets multiplied by its own shrinkage factor \(s_k\). When \(\rho_k \to 1\), \(s_k \to 1\) (keep the direction). When \(\rho_k \to 0\), \(s_k \to 0\) (drop it). The ratio p/n controls how aggressive the shrinkage is -- more parameters relative to samples means more shrinkage.

Shrinkage Spectrum
rho=0 (s->0) rho=0.5 rho=1 (s->1)

High canonical correlation = keep direction (s near 1). Low = shrink heavily (s near 0). Unlike RRR, there is no hard cutoff -- it's a continuous function of the canonical correlation strength.

Key Facts
  • Named after Breiman & Friedman (1997)
  • Shrinkage factor depends on both rho_k and the p/n ratio
  • Special case of a James-Stein-type estimator
  • More robust than RRR when rank is unknown
  • Gains are largest when n/p is small (high estimation variance)
