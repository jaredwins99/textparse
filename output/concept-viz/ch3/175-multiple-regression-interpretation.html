Left scatter: raw x₁ vs y — slope confounded by x₂. Right scatter (residualized): regress x₁ on x₂, take residuals z₁ = x₁ − x̂₁; regress y on x₂, take residuals r = y − ŷ. The slope of r on z₁ equals the multiple regression β̂₁ exactly.
Gram-Schmidt / Orthogonalization Algorithm (ESL §3.2.3)
Step 1: Regress x₁ on [1]: z₁ = x₁ − γ₁₀·1
Step 2: Regress x₂ on [1, z₁]: z₂ = x₂ − γ₂₀·1 − γ₂₁·z₁
Step p: Regress xₚ on [1, z₁, ..., z_{p-1}]: zₚ = xₚ − Σγⱼ·zⱼ
Final: β̂ₚ = ⟨zₚ, y⟩ / ⟨zₚ, zₚ⟩
The residual zₚ is orthogonal to all previous zⱼ, so β̂ₚ is the unique contribution of xₚ after removing all other predictors' influence.
Book Quotes
"The multiple regression coefficient β̂_j represents the additional contribution of x_j on y, after x_j has been adjusted for all other predictors." — ESL §3.2.3
"If x_p is orthogonal to the other predictors, then it has no effect on them, and the least squares coefficient of x_p is the same as the simple linear regression coefficient." — ESL §3.2.3
Statistical Properties
YES Causal interpretation only with RCT
YES Partial correlation interpretation
WARN Simple ≠ multiple when predictors correlated
NOTE Order-independent (unlike forward selection)
Simple regression β̂₁ = ρ_{xy}·(σy/σx). Multiple regression β̂₁ adjusts for shared variance with other predictors. The gap between them grows with predictor correlation.
