Canonical Correlation Analysis

Benefits

  • Finds the shared linear structure between two sets of variables -- the directions along which X and Y are most correlated
  • Canonical correlations are unique and ordered: tells you exactly how many meaningful shared dimensions exist
  • Foundation for reduced-rank regression: CCA directions become the projection that RRR uses
    "CCA finds uncorrelated linear combinations with successively maximized correlations." -- ESL §3.7
  • Rotation-invariant: result doesn't depend on how you parameterize X or Y

Tradeoffs

  • Requires n > p + q: breaks down in high dimensions. Sample canonical correlations are upward-biased when n is small relative to p + q
  • Only captures linear relationships -- nonlinear dependence is invisible
  • Canonical directions are not unique (only the correlations are) -- sign and scale are arbitrary
  • Not a prediction method by itself -- needs RRR or Curds & Whey to produce forecasts
    "CCA does not directly give a regression; it identifies the relevant low-dimensional subspace." -- ESL §3.7

Formula Reference
\(\displaystyle \max_{a,b}\; \mathrm{Corr}(\mathbf{X}a,\, \mathbf{Y}b) \quad \text{s.t.}\quad \mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{X}a)=\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{Y}b)=1\)
\(\displaystyle \rho_k = \sigma_k\!\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{xx}^{-1/2}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{xy}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{yy}^{-1/2}\right)\)

The canonical correlations are the singular values of the whitened cross-covariance matrix. The directions \(a_k, b_k\) are the corresponding left and right singular vectors, transformed back to the original coordinate systems.

Book Quote
"CCA finds uncorrelated linear combinations \(U_k = Xa_k\) and \(V_k = Yb_k\) with successively maximized correlations. The solution is obtained via a generalized SVD of the cross-covariance matrix."
-- ESL §3.7
Key Facts
  • Generalizes simple correlation to the multivariate setting
  • \(\rho_1 \geq \rho_2 \geq \cdots \geq \rho_K \geq 0\) always
  • Solved via SVD of the whitened cross-covariance
  • CCA with the first M directions is used in reduced-rank regression
  • Sample canonical correlations are biased upward when n is small
