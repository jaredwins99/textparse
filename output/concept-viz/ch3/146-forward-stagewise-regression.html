Forward Stagewise Regression

\(\hat{\beta}_j \leftarrow \hat{\beta}_j + \varepsilon \cdot \text{sign}\bigl(\langle r,\, x_j\rangle\bigr)\)  where \(j = \arg\max_k |\langle r,\, x_k \rangle|\), \(\;r = y - X\hat{\beta}\)

Adjust \(\varepsilon\) to see how step size controls regularization. Small \(\varepsilon\) mimics the LASSO path. Large \(\varepsilon\) approximates forward stepwise.
Book Quote
"At each step the algorithm identifies the variable most correlated with the current residual. It then computes the simple linear regression coefficient of the residual on this chosen variable, and adds a small amount \(\delta\) of it to its coefficient." — ESL §3.3.3, Hastie, Tibshirani & Friedman

Key insight: with \(\varepsilon \to 0\), the stagewise coefficient paths converge to the LASSO solution path. This connection was not recognized until the LARS algorithm (Efron et al., 2004).

Guarantees
~
Converges to OLS (ε → 0)
In the limit of infinitesimal steps, paths converge but require many iterations.
✓
Sparse / Regularized
Small ε gives sparse paths — many coefficients stay at zero for long, like LASSO.
✓
Monotone coefficient norms
\(\|\hat{\beta}\|_1\) increases monotonically — coefficients never go backwards.
✗
Low Variance
Discrete selection step still picks "most correlated" variable — variance from this choice.
✓
Works with p > n
No matrix inversion needed — updates one coefficient at a time. Usable in high dimensions.
