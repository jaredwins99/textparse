Lasso

Section 3.4 comparison / selection L1 Regularization

Shrinks coefficients toward zero via L1 penalty — some hit exactly zero, performing automatic variable selection.

Benefits & Tradeoffs
Why use Lasso, and what does it cost you?

Benefits

  • "Making t sufficiently small will cause some of the coefficients to be exactly zero" — automatic variable selection (ESL §3.4.2)
  • "The lasso does a kind of continuous subset selection" — produces sparse, interpretable models (ESL §3.4.2)
  • Convex objective — global optimum guaranteed
  • Equivalent to Bayesian MAP with Laplace (double-exponential) prior

Tradeoffs

  • Biased estimator — shrinks all coefficients toward zero (ESL §3.4.2)
  • "The lasso solution is not equivariant under re-scaling of the inputs" — must standardize predictors (ESL §3.4.2)
  • Selects at most N variables when p > N
  • No closed-form solution (requires iterative algorithms like LARS or coordinate descent)
"Because of the nature of the constraint, making t sufficiently small will cause some of the coefficients to be exactly zero. Thus the lasso does a kind of continuous subset selection." — Hastie, Tibshirani & Friedman, ESL Section 3.4.2
Limitations
When Lasso may not be the right choice
  • ! When p > N, selects at most N variables — use elastic net for grouped selection
  • ! Among a group of highly correlated predictors, tends to pick one arbitrarily
  • ! Coefficient estimates are biased — post-selection OLS (debiased lasso) can fix this
  • ! Standard inference (p-values, CIs) not directly available without special methods

Statistical Guarantees
Formal properties of the Lasso estimator
Unbiased? NO
Lasso shrinks all coefficients toward zero, introducing bias. The bias-variance tradeoff is favorable for prediction, but estimates are systematically biased away from the truth.
Consistent (Sign Recovery)? CONDITIONAL
Under the irrepresentability condition (non-zero predictors not too correlated with zero-coefficient predictors), Lasso recovers the true support as n → ∞. Otherwise sign recovery fails.
Prediction Consistent? YES
Under sparsity assumptions and appropriate λ choice (λ ~ √(log p / n)), Lasso achieves minimax-optimal prediction risk among sparse models.
Low Variance? YES
By regularizing and setting small coefficients to exactly zero, Lasso substantially reduces variance compared to OLS, especially in high-dimensional settings (p ≈ n or p ≫ n).
The Lasso Objective
Lagrangian (penalized) and constraint forms are equivalent via the Lagrange multiplier relationship
$$\hat{\beta}^{\text{lasso}} = \arg\min_{\beta} \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{N}\!\left(y_i - \beta_0 - \sum_{j=1}^{p} x_{ij}\beta_j\right)^{\!2} + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^{p}|\beta_j| \right\}$$
Equivalent constraint form: minimize RSS subject to $\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{p}|\beta_j| \leq t$
Coordinate-wise update (soft-thresholding): $\hat{\beta}_j \leftarrow S\!\left(\frac{\langle x_j, r_{-j}\rangle}{\langle x_j, x_j\rangle},\; \frac{\lambda}{2\langle x_j,x_j\rangle}\right)$ where $S(z,\gamma) = \text{sign}(z)(|z|-\gamma)_+$
