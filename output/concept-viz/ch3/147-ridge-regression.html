Ridge Regression

comparison / selection Section 3.4.1 L2 Regularization

Shrinks coefficients toward zero via L2 penalty, trading bias for lower variance.

Benefits & Tradeoffs
Benefits
  • "Ridge regression shrinks the regression coefficients by imposing a penalty on their size" — trades bias for substantially lower variance (ESL §3.4.1)
  • Shrinks principal components with small eigenvalues most aggressively — "directions in which the data have low variance are shrunk the most" (ESL §3.4.1)
  • Equivalent to Bayesian posterior mode with Gaussian prior: "the ridge estimate is the mode of the posterior distribution" (ESL §3.4.1)
  • Always has a unique closed-form solution: β̂ = (XᵀX + λI)⁻¹Xᵀy
  • Handles collinearity (rank deficiency) — λI regularizes XᵀX, ensuring invertibility
Tradeoffs
  • "The restriction to unbiased estimates is not necessarily a wise one" — biased but can have lower MSE (ESL §3.2.2)
  • Never zeros out coefficients — "ridge regression doesn't set coefficients exactly to zero" (ESL §3.4.2)
  • All predictors remain in the model, reducing interpretability
  • Requires choosing λ via cross-validation or analytical criterion
"Ridge regression shrinks the regression coefficients by imposing a penalty on their size. The ridge coefficients minimize a penalized residual sum of squares." — The Elements of Statistical Learning, Hastie, Tibshirani & Friedman

At low data signal, the likelihood is nearly flat — any β is roughly equally good. The prior $\lambda(\beta_1^2 + \beta_2^2)$ creates the only structure in the posterior, forming a bowl at the origin. As data signal increases, the likelihood develops its own minimum, and the prior merely nudges it toward zero. This is exactly why ridge regression = Bayesian MAP with Gaussian prior.

Statistical Guarantees
Key theoretical properties from ESL Chapter 3.
Unbiased? NO
Introduces bias proportional to λ. "The restriction to unbiased estimates is not necessarily a wise one."
Consistent? YES
Under standard conditions, consistent as N → ∞ provided λ/N → 0. Bias vanishes asymptotically.
Lower Prediction MSE? YES
Trades bias for variance; often improves total MSE = Bias² + Var vs OLS, especially under collinearity.
Low Variance? YES
"Ridge regression shrinks the coefficients of the principal components, shrinking more depending on the size of the corresponding eigenvalue."
Formulas
$$\hat{\beta}^{\text{ridge}} = \arg\min_{\beta} \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{N}\!\left(y_i - \beta_0 - \sum_{j=1}^{p} x_{ij}\beta_j\right)^{\!2} + \lambda\sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_j^2 \right\}$$

Closed form: $\hat{\beta} = (X^TX + \lambda I)^{-1}X^Ty$

SVD form: $X\hat{\beta}^{\text{ridge}} = \displaystyle\sum_{j} u_j \frac{d_j^2}{d_j^2+\lambda} u_j^T y$

Each principal component $u_j$ is shrunk by factor $d_j^2/(d_j^2+\lambda)$. Directions with small singular values $d_j$ are shrunk most aggressively.
